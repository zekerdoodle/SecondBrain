# The Science of Attraction & Individual Variation

*A comprehensive exploration of universal attractiveness, individual "types," and the science of what draws us to specific people*

---

## Executive Summary

Human attraction operates on multiple levels simultaneously: deeply evolved universal preferences that signal health and genetic fitness, combined with highly individual "types" shaped by genes, early experiences, and cultural learning. This report synthesizes research across evolutionary psychology, behavioral genetics, and developmental science to explain both why certain features are universally attractive and why your particular "type" differs from everyone else's.

**Key Findings:**
- Universal attractiveness markers (symmetry, averageness, certain ratios) are real and cross-culturally validated, though often overstated in pop science
- Individual variation in preferences has multiple causes: ~30% genetic, ~10% shared family environment, ~60% unique experiences
- Sexual imprinting on parental features is real but modest, and depends heavily on relationship quality
- MHC-based "chemistry" exists but is weaker than commonly believed and confounded by cultural factors
- "Type" plasticity exists but decreases with age; early experiences matter most

## Part 1: Universal Attractiveness Vectors

### What Features Are Universally Attractive?

Research has identified several facial and body characteristics that receive consistently high attractiveness ratings across diverse cultures. However, the evidence varies significantly in strength.

#### 1. Facial Averageness (Strong Evidence)

**What it is:** Faces that represent the mathematical average of a population are rated as more attractive than most individual faces. This can be demonstrated through "morphing" experiments where composite faces of many individuals are rated higher than the originals.

**Cross-cultural validation:** The preference for average faces has been documented across European populations, among the Hadza hunter-gatherers of Tanzania, and in Japanese, Chinese, and African populations. This is one of the most robust findings in attractiveness research.

**Why it's attractive (evolutionary basis):**
- **Developmental stability hypothesis:** Average features may signal that development proceeded without disruption from genetic mutations, pathogens, or environmental stressors
- **Cognitive processing fluency:** Average faces may be easier to process mentally, and we tend to prefer things that are easy to cognitively process
- **Population adaptation signal:** Features that are common in a population may indicate successful adaptation to local conditions

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★★★ (Very Strong)

[Sources: [Little, 2011 - Facial Attractiveness Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3130383/), [Rhodes et al., 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11430245/)]

---

#### 2. Facial Symmetry (Moderate Evidence, Recently Challenged)

**What it is:** The degree to which the left and right sides of a face mirror each other.

**The traditional view:** Symmetry was long considered a primary driver of attractiveness, supposedly signaling "developmental stability"—the ability to develop normally despite genetic mutations and environmental stressors.

**The complication:** Recent large-scale research challenges symmetry's importance. A 2023 study in *Evolution and Human Behavior* found that facial attractiveness is robustly predicted by only two characteristics: low distinctiveness (averageness) and femininity. Notably, facial symmetry showed "no robust effect" on attractiveness ratings when these other factors were controlled.

**Why the discrepancy?**
- Symmetry correlates with averageness, so earlier studies may have conflated the two
- Perfect symmetry can look "uncanny" and artificial
- Small asymmetries may be imperceptible or even add character

**Current scientific consensus:** Symmetry likely plays some role, but it's probably a weaker predictor than averageness or sexual dimorphism. The pop-science emphasis on symmetry appears overstated.

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★☆☆ (Moderate, Contested)

[Sources: [Holzleitner et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1090513823000879), [Rhodes, 2006](https://homepages.uc.edu/~martinj/Taste%20Food%20&%20Wine/Aesthetics_of_Food_&_Drink/Rhodes%20-%20Facial%20symmetry%20and%20the%20perception%20of%20beauty.pdf)]

---

#### 3. Sexual Dimorphism (Strong but Complex Evidence)

**What it is:** Features that differ between males and females—masculine features in men (strong jaw, brow ridge, larger nose) and feminine features in women (larger eyes relative to face, fuller lips, smaller chin).

**The finding:** Feminine female faces are consistently rated as more attractive across cultures. However, preferences for masculine male faces are highly variable and context-dependent.

**Why feminine features are attractive in women:**
- Signal youth and fertility
- Associated with higher estrogen levels
- Cross-culturally consistent

**Why masculine features in men are complicated:**
- High testosterone signals immunocompetence (only healthy males can afford the costly masculine phenotype)
- BUT masculine men are perceived as less trustworthy, more likely to be unfaithful, and less invested fathers
- Women's preferences for masculinity fluctuate with menstrual cycle, relationship context (short-term vs. long-term), and local pathogen prevalence

**The trade-off:** Women appear to solve this by preferring more masculine partners for short-term relationships and less masculine partners for long-term relationships—or by preferring intermediate masculinity overall.

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★★☆ (Strong, but nuanced)

[Sources: [Little et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3130383/), [Evolutionary Psychology of Facial Attractiveness](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16318594/)]

---

#### 4. Body Proportions: Waist-to-Hip Ratio (Strong but Variable Evidence)

**What it is:** The ratio of waist circumference to hip circumference. The commonly cited "ideal" for female attractiveness is approximately 0.70.

**Cross-cultural evidence:** Studies across the UK, Australia, Germany, Greece, India, Cameroon, Indonesia, Samoa, and New Zealand have found preferences for lower WHR in women.

**Why it's attractive:**
- WHR is sexually dimorphic (women have lower WHR than men in all studied populations)
- Low WHR correlates with higher estrogen and better fertility outcomes
- High WHR correlates with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and various cancers
- WHR predicts pregnancy success and reduced miscarriage risk

**The complications:**
- Hunter-gatherer populations (like the Hadza) sometimes prefer higher WHR, possibly because fat stores are more valuable when starvation risk is high
- BMI (overall weight) may be a stronger predictor than WHR in some contexts
- The "ideal" ratio varies somewhat across cultures and with local resource availability

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★★☆ (Strong, with cultural modulation)

[Sources: [Singh & Singh, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1090513809000889), [Frontiers Review 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6563790/)]

---

#### 5. Skin Quality and Coloration (Strong Evidence)

**What it is:** Skin color, texture, and apparent health as reflected in the skin.

**Key findings:**
- **Carotenoid coloration** (yellowish tones from fruits and vegetables) is rated as attractive and healthy-looking across cultures
- **Skin redness** (indicating blood oxygenation and cardiovascular health) enhances attractiveness
- **Skin luminance and evenness** signal youth and health
- Carotenoid coloration is preferred even over melanin-based tans

**Why it's attractive:**
- In many species, carotenoid-based coloration is a "handicap signal"—only healthy individuals can afford to divert carotenoids to ornamentation rather than immune function
- Skin quality provides an honest signal of current health, diet, and immune status
- Unlike symmetry or bone structure, skin is highly dynamic and reflects ongoing health

**This is arguably underappreciated in pop science discussions of attractiveness.**

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★★☆ (Strong)

[Sources: [Stephen et al., 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1090513810001169), [Lefevre & Perrett, 2015](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1080/17470218.2014.944194), [Foo et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7078114/)]

---

#### 6. Voice Pitch (Strong Evidence)

**What it is:** The fundamental frequency of the voice—lower/deeper for men, higher for women.

**Key findings:**
- Women generally prefer lower-pitched male voices
- Men generally prefer higher-pitched female voices
- Voice pitch is more sexually dimorphic in humans than in any other ape (about 60% difference vs. 8% for height)

**Why deep male voices are attractive:**
- Signal larger body size
- Correlate with testosterone levels
- Associated with physical dominance
- In hunter-gatherer populations, men with deeper voices have more children

**The complications (again, trade-offs):**
- Very low voices aren't always preferred—there's a curvilinear relationship
- Deep voices are associated with perceived infidelity risk
- Women prefer deeper voices more for short-term than long-term relationships
- The preference is stronger during the fertile phase of the menstrual cycle

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★★☆ (Strong)

[Sources: [Puts et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2391230/), [Aung & Puts, 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7719159/)]

---

#### 7. The "Golden Ratio" and Optimal Proportions (Moderate Evidence, Overstated in Pop Culture)

**What it is:** The idea that facial features conforming to the golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618) are optimally attractive.

**The reality:** While there's something to proportional harmony, the specific golden ratio appears less important than simpler "optimal" proportions. Research by Pamela Pallett and colleagues found that:
- Optimal vertical eye-mouth distance: ~36% of face length
- Optimal horizontal interocular distance: ~46% of face width

These "new golden ratios" happen to match the proportions of an average face—suggesting averageness, not the golden ratio per se, is what's being detected.

**Strength of evidence:** ★★★☆☆ (The general idea of proportional harmony has support; the specific golden ratio is likely overstated)

[Sources: [Pallett et al., 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2814183/)]

---

### Summary: The Evolutionary Logic

These universal preferences share a common evolutionary logic: they're all potential cues to:

1. **Genetic quality** (developmental stability, immunocompetence)
2. **Current health** (skin quality, body composition)
3. **Reproductive capacity** (sexual dimorphism, body proportions)
4. **Absence of heritable disease** (symmetry, averageness)

The "honest signal" framework explains why these preferences evolved: features that are difficult to fake and correlate with actual fitness became targets of sexual selection.

## Part 2: Individual Type Variation

If universal attractiveness vectors exist, why do individual "types" vary so dramatically? Your friend finds one person irresistible while you feel nothing—and vice versa. This section examines the major hypotheses for this variation.

### Hypothesis 1: MHC/Immune Compatibility ("Chemistry")

**The theory:** Individuals are attracted to partners with different Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) alleles, which would produce offspring with more diverse immune systems.

**The famous study:** Claus Wedekind's "sweaty t-shirt study" (1995) found that women preferred the body odor of men with dissimilar MHC genes—unless they were on hormonal contraceptives, which reversed the preference.

**What the evidence actually shows:**

- **Olfactory preferences exist:** Multiple studies confirm that people can detect MHC-related cues in body odor and have preferences
- **But actual mating patterns are unclear:** A meta-analysis by Winternitz et al. (2017) found that while humans show some preference for MHC-diverse mates, there's no consistent pattern of MHC-dissimilar mate choice in actual couples
- **Cultural confounds are huge:** Much of the apparent MHC-similarity in real couples may be due to ethnic homogamy (people marrying within their ethnic group for cultural reasons)
- **The effect is probably modest:** Even if MHC preferences exist, they appear to be one factor among many, not a dominant driver

**Current assessment:** MHC-based "chemistry" is real but probably much weaker than pop science suggests. It may influence initial attraction and provide a "veto" function (explaining strong negative reactions to some potential partners) rather than driving positive attraction.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★★☆☆☆ (Real but modest)

[Sources: [Wedekind et al., 1995](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.1995.0087), [Winternitz et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27859823/), [Havlicek & Roberts, 2009](https://www.nature.com/articles/6884280)]

---

### Hypothesis 2: Sexual Imprinting (Parental Features)

**The theory:** Individuals develop attraction templates based on the physical characteristics of their opposite-sex parent during a sensitive period in early childhood.

**What the evidence shows:**

- **The effect is real but modest:** Studies find that adopted daughters with positive relationships with their adoptive fathers are more likely to choose partners who physically resemble those fathers
- **Relationship quality matters critically:** Imprinting effects are much stronger when the parent-child relationship is warm and supportive. Negative relationships may even reverse the effect (negative imprinting)
- **Self-reported awareness:** About 15-19% of people report being consciously aware of attraction to parental-resembling features
- **Multiple sensitive periods:** Evidence suggests learning occurs both in early childhood (roughly 0-6 years) and again in adolescence

**Westermarck Effect (negative imprinting):** People raised in close proximity during early childhood (like kibbutz children or Taiwanese minor marriages) show sexual aversion to each other—even if genetically unrelated. This suggests the imprinting system can work in both directions.

**Current assessment:** Sexual imprinting contributes to individual variation but is not deterministic. It appears to create a "familiarity template" that, under positive emotional associations, biases attraction toward similar features.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★★★☆☆ (Real and meaningful, but not dominant)

[Sources: [Bereczkei et al., 2004](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.2003.2672), [Marcinkowska & Rantala, 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10480955/), [Rantala & Marcinkowska, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0191886921002713)]

---

### Hypothesis 3: Competition Diffusion / Frequency-Dependent Selection

**The theory:** If everyone preferred exactly the same features, competition for those partners would be extreme. Diversity in preferences may have evolved to "spread out" mate competition across the population.

**The evidence:**

- **Negative frequency-dependent selection** is well-documented in nature: rare phenotypes often have mating advantages precisely because they're novel or less competed-over
- **"Inferred attractiveness" hypothesis:** Recent research suggests people observe others' mate choices and learn to prefer distinguishing features—creating a dynamic where different traits cycle in and out of fashion
- **Rare-male advantage:** In guppies and other species, females preferentially mate with males that have unusual coloration

**In humans:**
- Some evidence for "novelty preferences" in mate choice
- Social learning can spread and maintain diverse preferences across populations
- Fashion effects in attractiveness judgments demonstrate cultural malleability

**Current assessment:** This hypothesis is theoretically compelling but under-studied in humans compared to other mechanisms. It likely plays some role in maintaining population-level diversity in preferences.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★★☆☆☆ (Plausible but understudied)

[Sources: [Pruitt et al., 2023 - Inferred Attractiveness](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.3002269), [Jones et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3013470/)]

---

### Hypothesis 4: Bet-Hedging (Species Resilience)

**The theory:** Diverse preferences across a population produce diverse offspring, which hedge against unpredictable environmental changes—similar to how some plants produce seeds with different germination requirements.

**The evidence:**

- **Theoretically sound:** Bet-hedging is well-established in evolutionary biology for many traits
- **In mate choice specifically?** A meta-analysis by Holman (2015) found that the fitness gains from bet-hedging via multiple mating are "meager" and unlikely to be a major driver of preference diversity
- **May work at population level:** Even if individual bet-hedging is weak, population-level diversity in preferences could emerge from other mechanisms while still providing resilience benefits

**Current assessment:** This is probably a consequence rather than a cause of preference diversity. The mechanisms that create diverse preferences (imprinting, learning, genetic variation) happen to also provide bet-hedging benefits, but that may not be why they evolved.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★☆☆☆☆ (Probably a byproduct, not a driver)

[Sources: [Holman, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26596684/)]

---

### Hypothesis 5: Genetic Variation in Preferences

**The theory:** Some variation in "type" is simply heritable—different people have genetically different preferences.

**What the evidence shows:**

Twin studies have directly measured this:
- **Heritability of preferences ranges from 0-57%** depending on the specific trait
- **Physical attractiveness preferences:** ~29% heritable in women
- **Height preferences:** Up to 57% heritable in men
- **Hair preferences:** Up to 48% heritable in women
- **Overall pattern:** "At least 10-30% of the variance in partner choice is due to genetic factors"

**What this means:** If identical twins have more similar "types" than fraternal twins (which they do), some of that similarity must be genetic. This supports the idea that preferences themselves, not just the features being evaluated, are under genetic influence.

**Current assessment:** This is one of the strongest documented contributors to individual variation. Genetic differences in preferences are real and substantial for some traits.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★★★★☆ (Strong evidence for meaningful genetic component)

[Sources: [Zietsch et al., 2012](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1558-5646.2011.01546.x), [Verweij et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3444291/)]

---

### Hypothesis 6: Assortative Mating (Like Attracts Like)

**The theory:** People are attracted to others similar to themselves.

**The evidence:**

- **Overwhelming support for similarity:** Partner correlations are "overwhelmingly in the positive direction" across nearly all studied traits
- **Strongest for:** Age, education, social attitudes, religiosity, height
- **Weaker for:** Personality traits
- **Not convergence:** Couples are similar from early in relationships; they don't become more similar over time

**Mechanisms:**
- Active preference for self-similarity
- "Social homogamy" (meeting similar people due to shared environments)
- Mating market dynamics (similar people are more accessible)

**Current assessment:** Assortative mating is extremely well-documented. However, it's somewhat separate from "type" in the usual sense—it's about preferring similarity to self rather than preferring a specific external ideal.

**Contribution to type variation:** ★★★★☆ (Strong and well-documented)

[Sources: [Luo, 2017](https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/spc3.12337), [Yengo et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8610703/)]

---

### The Overall Picture: Where Does "Type" Come From?

Based on twin studies and research synthesis, here's approximately how the variance breaks down:

| Factor | Estimated Contribution | Confidence |
|--------|----------------------|------------|
| Unique environmental experiences | ~60% | Medium-High |
| Genetic factors | ~10-30% | High |
| Shared family environment | ~10% | Medium |
| MHC/immune compatibility | Small | Medium |
| Sexual imprinting | Meaningful but variable | Medium |

**The big takeaway:** Most of the variation in individual "type" comes from unique, non-shared experiences—your specific life history, early crushes, media exposure, and the particular combination of experiences that shaped you. This is simultaneously reassuring (your type isn't genetically destined or determined by your parents) and frustrating (it's hard to identify what specifically caused it).

## Part 3: Formative Experiences and Attraction

### How Do Early Experiences Shape Adult Attraction?

#### Sexual Imprinting: The Details

**The sensitive period(s):**
- **Early childhood (roughly 0-6 years):** Primary period for learning parental features
- **Adolescence:** Secondary sensitive period, possibly for peer and media influences
- **The offset is unclear:** Research hasn't definitively established when the window closes

**What gets imprinted:**
- Facial features of opposite-sex parent (when relationship is positive)
- Possibly hair color, eye color, and other salient features
- The mechanism appears to be associative learning rather than a rigid template

**Modulating factors:**
- **Relationship quality is crucial:** Positive emotional bonds strengthen imprinting; negative relationships may create avoidance
- **Parental investment:** Children who received more emotional support from opposite-sex parents show stronger resemblance to those parents in partner choice
- **Not deterministic:** Imprinting creates biases, not requirements

[Sources: [Bereczkei et al., 2004](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.2003.2672), [Heffernan & Fraley, 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0191886921002713)]

---

#### Beyond Parental Imprinting: Other Formative Influences

**Early crushes and romantic experiences:**
- First romantic/sexual experiences may have lasting impact on preferences
- "Associative learning" can link arousal states with specific features encountered during those states
- This is less studied than parental imprinting but likely significant

**Media and cultural exposure:**
- Social learning research demonstrates that observing others' mate choices influences one's own preferences
- This can generate and maintain cultural differences in beauty standards
- May explain why preferences can shift across generations and between cultures

**Peer group influence:**
- Adolescent peer groups establish local norms for attractiveness
- "Mate-choice copying" (preferring partners that others find attractive) is documented in humans

---

### How Malleable Is "Type" Across a Lifetime?

**Evidence for plasticity:**
- Preferences can shift with:
  - Menstrual cycle phase (women's preferences for masculinity)
  - Relationship context (short-term vs. long-term)
  - Own attractiveness level (more attractive people have higher standards)
  - Local mating market conditions
  - Reproductive state (pregnancy changes preferences)

**Evidence for stability:**
- Core "type" appears relatively stable after adolescence
- Sexual imprinting effects persist into adulthood
- Genetic components of preference are, by definition, stable

**The overall picture:** "Type" has both stable components (genetic, early imprinted) and flexible components (context-dependent adjustments). The stable components appear to be established by early adulthood, while the flexible components allow ongoing calibration to circumstances.

## Part 4: Synescence Tie-In (Speculative Section)

*This section is deliberately speculative, exploring what would be required if an advanced intelligence wanted to engineer mutual physical attraction between two people. This is a thought experiment, not a prediction or recommendation.*

### The Engineering Challenge

Creating two people who would be each other's "perfect physical match" would require influencing both the universal attractiveness markers AND the individualized "type" system. Here's what an ASI would need to consider:

---

### A. Genetic Design Considerations

**For Universal Attractiveness (relatively straightforward):**

1. **Facial structure genes:** Select for variants associated with:
   - Population-average proportions (the "sweet spot")
   - Good developmental stability (avoiding asymmetry-promoting variants)
   - Appropriate sexual dimorphism for sex

2. **Body proportion genes:** Target:
   - Height within normal range (avoiding extremes)
   - WHR-influencing genes (fat distribution)
   - Skeletal proportion genes

3. **Skin quality genes:**
   - Carotenoid metabolism genes (ability to deposit carotenoids in skin)
   - Melanin distribution genes (evenness)
   - Collagen and aging-related genes

4. **Voice genes:**
   - Recently identified genetic variants affecting voice pitch
   - Larynx size determinants

**For Mutual Type-Matching (much harder):**

5. **MHC selection:**
   - Would need to ensure MHC dissimilarity between the two individuals
   - But NOT so dissimilar that other compatibility issues arise

6. **Preference heritability problem:**
   - Since preferences are partially heritable, you'd need to know what genes influence what preferences
   - Current research identifies heritability but not specific genes
   - Would need massive genetic association studies of preference variations

---

### B. Environmental/Experiential Design

This is where it gets truly complex. To create mutual imprinting:

**1. Designing Parental Phenotypes**

The ASI would need to ensure each person's caregivers physically resemble the future partner:
- Person A raised by caregiver(s) who resemble Person B
- Person B raised by caregiver(s) who resemble Person A
- Positive emotional relationships with those caregivers are essential

**Problem:** If both people are being created, you have a bootstrapping problem—you need to know what they'll look like to design their parental environments, but their phenotypes depend on genes you're selecting.

**Solution approaches:**
- Highly accurate phenotype prediction from genotype
- Iterative design process
- Using synthetic or selected caregivers who approximate target phenotypes

**2. Designing Early Experiences**

Beyond parental imprinting:
- Curated media exposure featuring each other's phenotype (or close approximations)
- Early peer groups including individuals with relevant features
- Positive emotional experiences associated with target features

**3. Timing the Sensitive Periods**

The ASI would need to:
- Deliver imprinting stimuli during the early childhood window (~0-6 years)
- Potentially reinforce during the adolescent sensitive period
- Ensure no competing imprinting on other individuals' features

---

### C. What Would the ASI Need to Know/Control?

**Minimum requirements:**

1. **Complete genotype-phenotype mapping** for:
   - Facial features
   - Body composition and proportions
   - Voice characteristics
   - Skin characteristics
   - Aging trajectories (to ensure continued attractiveness)

2. **Genotype-preference mapping:**
   - Which genetic variants influence which preferences
   - Currently not well characterized

3. **Developmental trajectory prediction:**
   - How will each person's appearance change from childhood through adulthood?
   - When will they be most/least attractive to each other?

4. **Control over early environment:**
   - Caregiver selection or creation
   - Media exposure control
   - Social environment design
   - Emotional relationship quality between child and caregivers

5. **Cultural context:**
   - What are the prevailing attractiveness norms?
   - How will social learning interact with the designed preferences?

---

### D. Fundamental Limitations and Challenges

**1. The prediction problem:**
Phenotype prediction from genotype is currently poor for complex traits. An ASI would need far better models than currently exist.

**2. The plasticity problem:**
Even with perfect design, life experiences outside the controlled period could shift preferences. Illness, trauma, significant relationships, and cultural shifts could all interfere.

**3. The measurement problem:**
"Attraction" isn't a single thing—physical attraction, romantic attachment, and sexual desire are partially separable. Optimizing for one doesn't guarantee the others.

**4. The dynamics problem:**
Attraction changes over time within relationships. Initial attraction doesn't predict sustained attraction. The ASI would need to consider decades-long trajectories.

**5. The complementarity vs. similarity tension:**
Research shows people prefer both similarity (assortative mating) and complementarity (MHC dissimilarity). These can conflict, requiring careful balancing.

**6. The reciprocity requirement:**
Creating Person A to be attractive TO Person B is different from creating Person A to find Person B attractive. Both directions require different interventions, and they must be coordinated.

---

### E. A Hypothetical Protocol

If an ASI attempted this, a rough protocol might be:

1. **Define target phenotypes** for both individuals, optimizing for:
   - Universal attractiveness markers
   - Mutual MHC compatibility
   - Mutual assortative matching (similar but not identical)
   - Complementary features where relevant

2. **Select/design genotypes** to produce these phenotypes with high confidence

3. **Design developmental environments** where:
   - Each individual's primary caregivers physically approximate the other's target phenotype
   - Positive attachment relationships are ensured
   - Media and cultural exposure reinforces target features
   - Social environments include appropriate peers

4. **Monitor and adjust** through childhood and adolescence:
   - Track actual phenotype development against predictions
   - Adjust environmental inputs as needed
   - Ensure no competing imprinting occurs

5. **Orchestrate meeting timing:**
   - Introduce the individuals when both are in optimal states
   - Ensure conditions favor positive first impressions
   - Leverage social proof and context effects

**The honest assessment:** This would require capabilities far beyond current science—both in predictive modeling and in environmental control. The number of variables and their interactions makes this an extraordinarily difficult optimization problem even for a superintelligence.

---

## Conclusions

### What We Know With High Confidence

1. **Universal attractiveness markers exist** and are cross-culturally validated: averageness, appropriate sexual dimorphism, skin quality, and voice pitch are real and evolutionarily grounded.

2. **Individual "type" variation is real** and substantial—not mere noise or cultural whim.

3. **Preferences are partially heritable** (10-30%), establishing that genetic differences in attractiveness assessments exist.

4. **Sexual imprinting occurs** and is modulated by relationship quality with caregivers.

5. **Preferences show some plasticity** but become more stable after early adulthood.

### What Remains Uncertain

1. **The specific genes** influencing preferences are largely unidentified.

2. **The exact sensitive periods** for various types of preference learning.

3. **The relative importance** of different mechanisms varies across individuals and is hard to quantify.

4. **How preference components interact** and potentially conflict.

### Key Takeaways

- Your "type" is a complex product of genes you inherited, features you encountered during sensitive developmental periods, and a large dose of idiosyncratic experience.
- Universal attractiveness exists but is weaker than pop science suggests—there's enormous individual variation overlaid on modest universal tendencies.
- The system is partially malleable, partially fixed—shaped by evolution to be responsive to local conditions while maintaining beneficial universal heuristics.
- Engineering "perfect" mutual attraction would be extraordinarily difficult, requiring deep understanding and control of both genetic and developmental factors across two individuals simultaneously.
